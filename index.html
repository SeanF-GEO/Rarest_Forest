69 rare forest ecoregions across 20 biodiversity hotspots, identified by the smallest extent of forest cover within protected areas.

This map visualizes the world's rarest and least protected forests located within global biodiversity hotspots — regions that hold exceptional concentrations of endemic species yet face severe habitat loss. Using WWF ecoregion boundaries, ESA GlobCover satellite land cover data, and the IUCN World Database on Protected Areas, researchers identified 276 forest ecoregions across 32 hotspots. The bottom quartile — 69 ecoregions with the smallest extent of forest in protected areas — are shown here as the world's rarest forests.

Of these 69 rare ecoregions, 45 are located on islands or island archipelagos, and 47 contain less than 10% of their forest within any protected area. Four ecoregions stand out as the most critically vulnerable: San Félix-San Ambrosio Islands, Tubuai, Maldives-Lakshadweep-Chagos Archipelago, and Yap. Each covers less than 500 km², retains less than 50 km² of forest, and has zero formally designated protected areas. These small island forests face compounding threats from invasive species, development, fire, and climate-driven sea level rise — making them urgent priorities for conservation action.

Gillespie, T. W., Lipkin, B., Sullivan, L., Benowitz, D. R., Pau, S., & Keppel, G. (2012). The rarest and least protected forests in biodiversity hotspots. Biodiversity and Conservation, 21(14), 3597–3611.
